# China Deepfake Law Summary

## Core Legal Framework
- The `2022 Deep Synthesis Provisions` form the core governance layer for synthetic-content services, structuring platform duties around moderation, labeling, traceability, and cooperation under administrative online-information regulation. (Ref1, Ref2)
- The `2025 labeling notice` further clarifies implementation direction for AI-generated/synthetic-content marking obligations. (Ref4)
- China’s model is best understood as an administrative governance architecture combined with public-order enforcement channels, rather than a criminal-only deepfake model. (Ref1, Ref2, Ref4)

## Summary
- The strongest feature in the current China corpus is codified provider/platform governance: labeling, traceability, and compliance cooperation are embedded as ongoing operational duties, not only post-incident penalties. (Ref1, Ref2)
- Operational risk therefore depends heavily on system-level controls (identification, marking, logging, enforcement-response workflows), not only isolated legal interpretation of single incidents. (Ref1, Ref4)

## Policy Dimensions
- `Labeling status`: labeling is one of the clearest codified dimensions in the China set. The 2022 provisions establish the baseline governance obligations, and the 2025 official notice strengthens implementation direction for generated/synthetic-content marking. Ref5 (proposed guidance) is non-final, so current-force conclusions should rely primarily on Ref1/Ref2/Ref4. (Ref1, Ref2, Ref4, Ref5)
- `Election-related status`: current corpus does not provide clear evidence of one stand-alone election-deepfake statute. A conservative interpretation is that election-related synthetic harms are controlled through broader information-order, misinformation, and public-order regulatory channels. (Ref1, Ref2)
- `Non-consensual deepfake status`: non-consensual synthetic harms appear to be governed through combined administrative/public-order/cyber enforcement pathways rather than one isolated deepfake offense chapter in this corpus. Practical response still requires rapid operational controls (blocking, logging, cooperation requests) due to speed-of-harm dynamics. (Ref1, Ref2)
- `Service-provider/platform liability status`: this is the structurally strongest codified area. Platform obligations on content management, labeling, identity/traceability, and removal/cooperation are embedded in administrative governance logic, making execution quality of internal workflows central to compliance outcomes. (Ref1, Ref2, Ref4)
- `Penalty/sanctions codification status`: sanctions follow a layered pathway combining administrative enforcement and criminal/public-order channels. Teams should manage exposure through stage-based enforcement logic (violation -> order -> non-compliance -> escalation), with latest-publication cross-checks for article-level precision. (Ref1, Ref2)

## Practice Notes (by Dimension)
- `Labeling`: audit not only presence of labels but placement, persistence, and user-recognition quality.
- `Election`: treat election/public-interest synthetic content as a separate high-risk governance stream.
- `Non-consensual deepfakes`: maintain one-pass SOPs for intake, immediate action, reupload detection, and evidence chain retention.
- `Service-provider/platform liability`: keep policy controls and action logs trace-linked for regulator-facing accountability.
- `Penalty`: run a unified sanctions matrix covering administrative stage and potential criminal/public-order escalation.

## Verify Before Use
- Ref5 is proposed/non-final and should not be used alone for current-force conclusions; prioritize Ref1/Ref2/Ref4 and recheck against latest official publications.
