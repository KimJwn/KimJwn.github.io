# EU Deepfake Law Summary

## Core Legal Framework
- `EU AI Act`: the central EU regulation establishing transparency duties for AI-generated/manipulated content and broader AI governance architecture. (Ref1)
- `DSA`: the core platform-governance regulation imposing systemic-risk mitigation, illegal-content handling, and transparency obligations. (Ref2)
- The EU model is not a single criminal deepfake code; it is a layered system combining EU-level regulatory duties with member-state criminal/election-law enforcement. (Ref1, Ref2)

## Summary
- The strongest EU feature is ex-ante governance: transparency, risk mitigation, and accountability duties are codified at regulation level for services/platforms. Direct criminalization remains largely member-state-driven. (Ref1, Ref2)
- Correct interpretation therefore requires separating EU-level obligations from member-state enforcement and designing operations to satisfy both layers in parallel. (Ref1, Ref2)

## Policy Dimensions
- `Labeling status`: labeling/transparency is comparatively well codified at EU level. The AI Act provides baseline obligations for generated/manipulated content transparency, while DSA risk-mitigation duties reinforce practical distinguishability and user-facing accountability in platform contexts. This operates as a combined model of content-transparency duty plus platform-operational duty. (Ref1, Ref2)
- `Election-related status`: no single stand-alone EU deepfake-election criminal statute is evident in current corpus. Governance is more realistically a combination of AI Act/DSA baseline obligations and member-state election-law enforcement, so election compliance must be jurisdiction-layered by member state. (Ref1, Ref2)
- `Non-consensual deepfake status`: non-consensual deepfake criminal enforcement is primarily member-state criminal-law territory, while EU-level rules supplement through platform transparency, response, and data-protection obligations. Incident handling must therefore integrate both member-state criminal pathways and EU regulatory duties. (Ref1)
- `Service-provider/platform liability status`: this is among the strongest codified dimensions in the EU set. DSA structures systemic risk governance, illegal-content response, and transparency reporting, and these obligations can intensify when combined with AI Act governance expectations. Practical compliance hinges on execution traceability, not policy text alone. (Ref2, Ref1)
- `Penalty/sanctions codification status`: EU does not provide one unified criminal deepfake penalty code; sanction exposure is layered across EU regulatory enforcement and member-state criminal channels. Effective risk management should track both EU-level and member-state-level sanction pathways simultaneously. (Ref1, Ref2)

Ref1: `sources/official/EU/EU-2025-EU_AI_ACt.pdf`
Ref2: `sources/official/EU/eu-2022-dsa.pdf`

## Practice Notes (by Dimension)
- `Labeling`: require fixed product controls for disclosure wording, placement, persistence, and user-recognition quality.
- `Election`: maintain member-state-specific election compliance profiles instead of one EU-wide generic policy.
- `Non-consensual deepfakes`: run dual-track incident SOPs that pair EU obligations with member-state criminal reporting/preservation workflows.
- `Service-provider/platform liability`: keep DSA governance documentation and operational action logs tightly linked for regulator-facing audits.
- `Penalty`: operate a dual-layer sanctions matrix covering EU regulatory exposure and member-state criminal exposure.
