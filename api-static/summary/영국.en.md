# UK Deepfake Law Summary

## Core Legal Framework
- `Online Safety Act 2023` (official statute): the core legal layer imposing risk-assessment, harmful-content response, and enforcement-cooperation duties on service providers. (Ref1)
- `Ofcom/government-linked reports and guidance`: operational support materials for AI labeling practices, user-facing notice design, and implementation constraints. (Ref2, Ref3, Ref4, Ref5)
- The UK model is primarily an OSA-centered platform-safety architecture that absorbs deepfake harms into broader online-safety duties, rather than relying on a single stand-alone deepfake statute. (Ref1)

## Summary
- The UK regime is strongest at the provider-duty layer. Instead of relying only on offense-by-offense criminal codification, it places substantial compliance burden on services to assess, mitigate, and respond to synthetic-harm risks. (Ref1)
- For labeling, the practical regime is mixed: hard-law duties under OSA are combined with softer but operationally important implementation materials (research/technical/toolkit outputs). (Ref2, Ref3, Ref4, Ref5)

## Policy Dimensions
- `Labeling status`: under current official local resources, OSA does not cleanly appear as one broad stand-alone deepfake-labeling clause for all contexts. Labeling implementation is instead reinforced through Ofcom/technical research materials, so teams should separate what is legally mandatory from what is operationally recommended. (Ref1, Ref2, Ref3, Ref4)
- `Election-related status`: explicit codification of a single election-deepfake-only statute remains limited in the current local corpus. Election-related claims should therefore be framed conservatively and cross-checked against election-specific legal instruments beyond the current OSA-centered set. (Ref1)
- `Non-consensual deepfake status`: non-consensual intimate/sexual synthetic harms are treated as high-priority enforcement categories in practice. Effective compliance requires coupling legal policy with rapid operational response (intake, takedown, evidence retention, recirculation controls). (Ref1, Ref5)
- `Service-provider/platform liability status`: this is the strongest codified area in the UK set. Statutory provider duties on risk management, harmful-content response, and enforcement cooperation are structured at law level, with guidance materials adding implementation detail. Compliance quality therefore depends on end-to-end workflow maturity, not policy text alone. (Ref1, Ref2, Ref3, Ref5)
- `Penalty/sanctions codification status`: sanctions operate as a layered path across offense and enforcement channels, rather than one single penalty clause. Deepfake-subtype mapping still needs latest publication checks, but operationally the major risk driver is escalation after non-compliance with required corrective actions. (Ref1)

## Practice Notes (by Dimension)
- `Labeling`: govern not only wording but also placement, persistence, and user-recognition quality through measurable checks.
- `Election`: run election-period synthetic-content controls as a separate governance gate from general moderation flows.
- `Non-consensual deepfakes`: maintain SOPs that combine immediate blocking, reupload detection, and evidentiary logging.
- `Service-provider/platform liability`: align risk-assessment documents with action logs so regulators can trace policy-to-execution in one chain.
- `Penalty`: manage escalation scenarios (initial breach, post-notice non-compliance, repeat breach) in a dedicated sanctions-risk matrix.
