# Australia Deepfake Law Summary

## Core Legal Framework
- `Criminal Code Amendment (Deepfake Sexual Material) Act 2024 (C2024A00078)`: the principal criminal-law anchor that directly strengthens codification for non-consensual sexual deepfake harms. (Ref1)
- `Online Safety ... Industry Standard 2024 (F2024L00710)`: operational compliance instrument for service-provider obligations in online safety enforcement contexts. (Ref2)
- `Criminal Code Act 1995 Compilation No. 171 (Includes amendments: Act No. 2, 2026)`: consolidated legal context showing how amended criminal provisions sit in the broader code structure. (Ref4, Ref5, Ref6)

## Summary
- Australia’s model is not a single broad deepfake omnibus law. It is better read as a combined structure: targeted criminal codification for high-risk harm categories (especially non-consensual sexual synthetic material) plus online-safety provider obligations under legislative instruments. (Ref1, Ref2)
- In practice, risk control works only when these two layers are managed together: offense-layer exposure and platform-operation-layer compliance. (Ref1, Ref2, Ref4)

## Policy Dimensions
- `Labeling status`: based on current corpus, a single universal deepfake-labeling mandate is not cleanly separated as one broad statutory clause across all contexts. Labeling-related operational expectations are instead mediated through online-safety and industry-standard obligations, meaning implementation quality depends on policy-design and workflow controls rather than a single standalone labeling section. (Ref2, Ref3)
- `Election-related status`: current official local materials provide limited direct evidence of one standalone federal election-deepfake statute. Election-related governance should therefore be stated conservatively and interpreted through interaction with broader online-safety and existing election/misinformation enforcement channels. (Ref2)
- `Non-consensual deepfake status`: this is the strongest codified dimension in the Australian set. The 2024 criminal-code amendment provides explicit offense-strengthening for non-consensual sexual deepfake harms, making this area comparatively clear at federal criminal-law level. (Ref1, Ref4)
- `Service-provider/platform liability status`: provider liability is operationally structured under the online-safety regime and associated standards. Effective compliance requires end-to-end controls for intake, classification, action, record-keeping, and reporting, not only high-level policy statements. (Ref2, Ref3)
- `Penalty/sanctions codification status`: sanctions are clearest in the non-consensual sexual deepfake track through criminal-law codification, while consolidated volumes help trace broader legal placement and amendment context. Operationally, teams should manage escalation risk across first breach, non-compliance with required actions, and repeat behavior. (Ref1, Ref4, Ref5, Ref6)

## Practice Notes (by Dimension)
- `Labeling`: enforce release checks for notice wording, placement, and user-recognition quality, with auditable evidence logs.
- `Election`: run election-period synthetic-content governance as a separate review gate from standard moderation.
- `Non-consensual deepfakes`: maintain SOPs for rapid block, reupload detection, and evidence retention (URL/hash/timestamp chain).
- `Service-provider/platform liability`: keep policy artifacts and action logs trace-linked so regulator/audit responses are immediate.
- `Penalty`: maintain an escalation matrix for first breach, post-notice non-compliance, and repeat breach scenarios.
