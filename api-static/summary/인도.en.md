# India Deepfake Law Summary

## Core Legal Framework
- `IT Rules` are the primary codified anchor in current corpus, structuring intermediary/platform obligations such as notice intake, takedown response, grievance handling, and recordkeeping. (Ref1)
- Deepfake-specific direction is visible in draft notice and explanatory materials, but final notified-text status must be tracked separately. (Ref2, Ref3)
- The present India profile is therefore best read as a transition model: `existing IT/intermediary framework + deepfake-specific refinement in progress`. (Ref1, Ref2)

## Summary
- The most stable legal layer in current corpus is intermediary compliance obligations. Deepfake-specific granularity remains partly draft-driven, so strong claims should be gated by final-publication verification. (Ref1, Ref2, Ref3)
- Operationally, teams should first harden current enforceable intermediary workflows and then maintain rapid update pathways for draft-to-final deepfake-specific rule transitions. (Ref1)

## Policy Dimensions
- `Labeling status`: labeling direction is visible in official draft/explanatory materials, but a fully finalized nationwide comprehensive mandate is not yet certain from current corpus alone. Services should run proactive labeling controls now while keeping a legal-update mapping layer ready for final notification text and commencement details. (Ref1, Ref2, Ref3)
- `Election-related status`: explicit stand-alone election-deepfake codification remains limited in current resources. Election-risk handling should be managed conservatively through broader intermediary obligations and related public-communication/misinformation enforcement channels until dedicated official election-text coverage is expanded. (Ref1, Ref2)
- `Non-consensual deepfake status`: non-consensual deepfake harms appear governed through combined intermediary compliance and existing criminal-law pathways. Effective risk control requires immediate operational response controls (rapid restriction, evidence chain retention, recirculation suppression) alongside legal interpretation. (Ref1, Ref2)
- `Service-provider/platform liability status`: this is the strongest currently codified dimension in India corpus. Intermediary obligations on intake, actioning, grievance process, and records are comparatively clear, so compliance quality depends on workflow consistency and auditability rather than policy text alone. (Ref1)
- `Penalty/sanctions codification status`: sanction pathways are distributed across existing legal channels, while deepfake-specific finalized article mapping remains in transition. Current best framing is: enforceable channels exist, but subtype-level sanction precision should be refreshed after final notification updates. (Ref1, Ref2)

## Practice Notes (by Dimension)
- `Labeling`: maintain internal disclosure controls now and keep configuration ready for rapid legal-text remapping when final notices are published.
- `Election`: process election-context synthetic media under a separate high-risk governance stream.
- `Non-consensual deepfakes`: enforce one-pass SOPs for intake, immediate action, evidence preservation, and recirculation control.
- `Service-provider/platform liability`: keep grievance/action logs in one auditable chain for regulator-facing accountability.
- `Penalty`: refresh sanctions matrices at each draft-to-final legal transition and map incident types to relevant legal pathways.

## Verify Before Use
- Recheck final notified text, commencement dates, and article wording for deepfake-specific amendments/rules against latest official publications.
